Overview

In an independent study with Paul Pangaro I created a concept map on the word joy. I first became interested in concept mapping after learning about the technique in a studio class. Concept maps such as the Java Technology Map by Audrey Crane and A Model of Play by the Dubberly Design Office captivated me with their intricate clarity and thorough consideration of all things.

Process

To create this map, I researched concept maps that originated from Novak & Gowin’s work about concept mapping for education. I then proceeded with Hugh Dubberly's approach to making concept maps. Below are iterations of the map from the first sketches to the final two versions. Between each version, I used the map as a conversational object with others and in particular with Professor Pangaro to further develop the map which is why you'll see comments on some of the maps in different colors.

Reflection

The greatest takeaway from this independent study for me comes from an article I read titled Disorder/Order: Discovery or Invention? by Heinz von Foerster. In this article, Foerster gives the series 8, 5, 4, 9, 1, 7, 6, 10, 3, 2 and the question - how are these numbers ordered? One may say randomly, others may join me in trying to find some arithmetic pattern. However, what may seem like randomness in one language is actually order in another. Notice that when each number is spelled out, the series is in alphabetical order - eight, five, four, nine, one, seven, six, ten, three, two. In the same way, concept maps allow designers to take systems and put them into a language in which order and simplicity can be found. By this, I am compelled as a designer to think that simplicity and understanding is possible for some of the most complex problems, it all just comes down to the how you look at it.
